Venture Capital

Let's learn about venture capital with Larry

Larry’s Lemonade: Funding from Victor

Business is booming at Larry’s Lemonade! For the first few weeks after opening, his customer base is growing rapidly! However, after two months, he plateaus. Business starts to die down, and he starts selling fewer and fewer cups of lemonade. He figures out that he can only make one cup of lemonade per minute, since he has to hand-squeeze each lemon and mix it himself. People don’t like standing in line when it's hot outside, so he loses a lot of customers who don’t want to wait in the hot sun! Larry determines that if he purchases the Juicer 5000, he can make 10 cups of lemonade per minute. That way he can sell 10 times as much lemonade and people won’t have to wait in line for so long! The only issue is the Juicer 5000 costs $100,000, and Larry simply can’t afford that. He gives his friend Victor a call. Victor works at a Venture Capital firm. Venture Capitalists invest in startups, in exchange for equity ("partial ownership") of the company. Victor’s firm currently has $10 million available to invest in start-ups, and it is Victor’s job to ensure that each investment has a high probability for a good ROI (return on investment). Most VC firms invest anywhere from half a million up to $10 million per company. Victor interviews Larry, asking him lots of questions about his business. For example, Victor asks about his business plan and his company's current and future profitability. He tries to get a feel for how committed Larry is and if Larry possesses the capabilities to lead the company. Victor agrees to invest $100,000 in Larry’s Lemonade in return for 50% equity, meaning that he will be partners with Larry, and they both get equal say in all business matters. Luckily, the Juicer 5000 is a huge hit, and sales skyrocket. Both Victor and Larry profit. While Larry gave up 50% ownership of his company to Victor, the money allows him to grow and expand in ways he wouldn't have been able to before.

Venture Capital in a Nutshell

  • Venture Capitalists finance start-ups and small businesses that they believe have long-term growth potential, usually in tech
  • They usually trade a set amount of funding for a percentage of equity in the company
  • A Venture Capitalist once said that of 20 companies in his fund, he expects one to be a homerun, 4 or 5 to be good returns, and the rest to fail
  • VC firms usually invest no more than a few million dollars in one company, and generally don’t take more than 49% equity

Why engineers in VC?

Engineers are great critical thinkers, and they love data. A major facet of venture capitalism is figuring out which startups are worth investing in -- that means organizing and analyzing data, and then coming to a conclusion. Excel is a must, which is perfect since Matlab is 10 times harder ;)
